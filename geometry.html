Unit 1: Intro to area and perimeter

Area formula intuition

Area measures how much space a shape covers. Counting unit squares helps build intuition for why formulas work.

Example: A 3 by 4 rectangle covers 12 unit squares, so area is 12.

Area and distributive property

Breaking a shape into parts shows area is additive. This matches the distributive property.

Example: A 3 by 7 rectangle can be split into 3 by 5 and 3 by 2: 21 = 15 + 6.
Practice:
1) Split 4 by 9 into 4 by 6 and 4 by 3: total area

Perimeter

Perimeter is the distance around a shape. Add the lengths of all sides.

Example: A 3 by 4 rectangle has perimeter 2(3 + 4) = 14.

Comparing area and perimeter

Shapes can have the same area but different perimeters, or the same perimeter but different areas.

Example: A 2 by 6 rectangle and a 3 by 4 rectangle both have area 12 but different perimeters.

Unit 2: Mass and volume

Mass

Mass measures the amount of matter in an object. It is commonly measured in grams or kilograms.

Example: A textbook might have a mass of about 1 kg.

Volume

Volume measures the space inside a 3D object. It is measured in cubic units.

Example: A box 2 by 3 by 4 has volume 24 cubic units.

Unit 3: Measuring angles

Intro to angles

An angle is formed by two rays with a common endpoint called the vertex. Angles measure rotation.

Example: A quarter turn is a 90 degree angle.

Angle types

Angles are classified by their measure. Understanding angle types is essential for analyzing shapes and geometric relationships.

  • Acute angle: Measures between 0° and 90°
  • Right angle: Measures exactly 90° (often marked with a small square)
  • Obtuse angle: Measures between 90° and 180°
  • Straight angle: Measures exactly 180° (forms a straight line)
  • Reflex angle: Measures between 180° and 360°
Example: 30° is acute; 90° is right; 120° is obtuse; 180° is straight.

Angle measurement

Angles are measured in degrees using a protractor. Full rotation is 360 degrees.

Example: Straight line angle is 180 degrees.

Unit 4: Plane figures

Quadrilaterals

Quadrilaterals are four-sided polygons. They are classified by their sides (parallel, equal, or perpendicular) and angles. Understanding relationships between quadrilateral types helps organize geometric properties.

Main types of quadrilaterals:

  • Square: All sides equal, all angles 90°, opposite sides parallel
  • Rectangle: Opposite sides equal, all angles 90°, opposite sides parallel
  • Rhombus: All sides equal, opposite sides parallel, opposite angles equal
  • Parallelogram: Opposite sides parallel and equal, opposite angles equal
  • Trapezoid: Exactly one pair of parallel sides (or at least one pair for inclusive definition)
  • Kite: Two pairs of adjacent sides equal

Hierarchy: A square is a special rectangle and a special rhombus. A rectangle and rhombus are special parallelograms.

Example: A rectangle has opposite sides parallel and equal, with all angles 90°. A square is a rectangle where all sides are also equal.

Classifying triangles

Triangles are classified in two ways: by their sides and by their angles. Understanding triangle classification helps identify properties and solve problems.

By side lengths:

  • Equilateral: All three sides equal; all three angles are 60°
  • Isosceles: Two sides equal; two angles equal (base angles)
  • Scalene: All three sides different; all three angles different

By angles:

  • Acute triangle: All three angles less than 90°
  • Right triangle: One angle equals 90°
  • Obtuse triangle: One angle greater than 90°
Example: A 3-4-5 triangle is a right triangle (one 90° angle) and scalene (all sides different). An equilateral triangle is automatically acute because each angle is 60°.

Parallel and perpendicular

Parallel lines never intersect. Perpendicular lines intersect at right angles.

Example: The x and y axes are perpendicular.

Classifying geometric shapes

Shapes are classified by number of sides, lengths, angles, and symmetry.

Example: A square is a rectangle and a rhombus.

Lines of symmetry

A line of symmetry splits a figure into two mirror-image halves.

Example: A square has four lines of symmetry.

Properties of shapes

Properties include side lengths, angle measures, parallel sides, and symmetry. These help identify shapes.

Example: A rectangle has four right angles.

Estimating length

Converting units of mass and volume

Converting units of length

Example: 2.5 m = 250 cm.

Unit 6: Volume

Volume with unit cubes

Volume can be found by counting unit cubes that fill a space. Each cube is one cubic unit.

Example: A 2 by 2 by 2 cube has volume 8.

Volume of rectangular prisms

Volume of a rectangular prism is length x width x height.

Example: A 3 by 4 by 5 prism has volume 60.

Unit 7: Coordinate plane

Intro to coordinate plane

The coordinate plane uses perpendicular axes to locate points with ordered pairs (x, y).

Example: The point (2, -1) is 2 right and 1 down.

Points in all four quadrants

The plane is divided into four quadrants based on the signs of x and y.

Example: (-3, 4) is in Quadrant II.

Unit 8: Decomposing to find area

Area of parallelograms

Area = base x height. The height is perpendicular to the base.

Example: Base 6, height 4 gives area 24.

Area of triangles

Area = 1/2 x base x height.

Example: Base 10, height 6 gives area 30.

Area and perimeter on the coordinate plane

Use distance between points for side lengths, then apply area or perimeter formulas.

Example: A rectangle with vertices (0,0), (4,0), (4,3), (0,3) has area 12.

Area of composite figures

Break complex shapes into simpler shapes, then add or subtract areas.

Example: Find area of a shape made of a rectangle plus a triangle.

Unit 9: 3D figures

Rectangular prism volume

Volume = length x width x height for rectangular prisms.

Example: 2 by 3 by 7 prism has volume 42.

Surface area

Surface area is the total area of all faces and surfaces of a 3D shape. It measures how much material you need to wrap or cover the shape. Different shapes have different formulas based on their geometry.

  • Rectangular prism (box): SA = 2(lw + lh + wh)
  • Cube (special prism): SA = 6s² (where s = side length)
  • Cylinder: SA = 2πr² + 2πrh (two circular bases plus lateral surface)
  • Sphere: SA = 4πr²

Relationship: Volume and surface area scale differently. If you double all dimensions, volume increases by 2³ = 8 times, but surface area increases by 2² = 4 times.

Example: A cube with side 2 has surface area 6(2)² = 24 square units. A cube with side 4 has surface area 6(4)² = 96 square units (4 times larger, even though volume went from 8 to 64).

Unit 10: Circles, cylinders, cones, and spheres

Area and circumference of circles

Circles have two fundamental measurements: circumference (the distance around) and area (the space inside). Both formulas depend on the radius r or diameter d (where d = 2r).

  • Circumference: C = 2πr or C = πd (distance around the circle)
  • Area: A = πr² (space inside the circle)

Important: Always use the radius (not diameter) when calculating area. Common approximation: π ≈ 3.14 or 22/7.

Example: A circle with radius 3:
  • Circumference = 2π(3) = 6π ≈ 18.85
  • Area = π(3)² = 9π ≈ 28.27
Practice:
1) Circle with radius 5 has area 25π and circumference 10π. (Are both correct?)

Area and circumference of fractions of circles

When you need the area or circumference of only part of a circle, use the fraction of the circle to adjust the full circle formulas. The fraction is typically measured as a central angle.

  • Arc length: For a central angle θ (in degrees): Arc length = (θ/360°) × 2πr
  • Sector area: For a central angle θ: Sector area = (θ/360°) × πr²

Intuition: A quarter circle (90°) has 1/4 the arc length and area of the full circle. A semicircle (180°) has 1/2.

Example: A sector with central angle 60° and radius 6:
  • Arc length = (60°/360°) × 2π(6) = (1/6) × 12π = 2π
  • Sector area = (60°/360°) × π(6)² = (1/6) × 36π = 6π

Volume of cylinders, spheres and cones

3D shapes formed from circles have volume formulas that extend 2D circle concepts. Each uses radius (not diameter) as the key measurement.

  • Cylinder: V = πr²h (area of circular base times height)
  • Cone: V = (1/3)πr²h (one-third the volume of a cylinder with same base and height)
  • Sphere: V = (4/3)πr³ (related to the sphere's radius cubed)

Intuition: A cone fits exactly 3 times into a cylinder of the same base and height. A sphere is roughly 2π/3 times a cube circumscribed around it.

Example: A cone with radius r = 3 and height h = 9:
  • Volume = (1/3)π(3)²(9) = (1/3)π(81) = 27π ≈ 84.8 cubic units
  • Compare to a cylinder with same dimensions: V = π(3)²(9) = 81π

Unit 11: Angle relationships

Vertical, complementary, and supplementary angles

When two lines or angles intersect or combine, they create special relationships. Recognizing these relationships helps solve for unknown angle measures.

  • Vertical angles: Formed when two lines intersect. Non-adjacent angles are equal. Always come in two pairs.
  • Complementary angles: Two angles that sum to 90°. Each angle is the complement of the other.
  • Supplementary angles: Two angles that sum to 180°. Each angle is the supplement of the other. Form a linear pair.

Important property: Vertical angles are always equal. If you know one angle formed by two intersecting lines, you can find all four angles.

Example: If two angles form a straight line and one is 110°, the other is 180° - 110° = 70° (supplementary). If two lines intersect and one angle is 55°, the vertical angle opposite it is also 55°, and the adjacent angles are each 125°.

1) If an angle is 35°, its complement is °

Missing angle problems

Use angle relationships to set up equations for unknown measures.

Example: If angles are complementary and one is x, the other is 90 - x.

Parallel lines and transversals

Transversals create angle pairs like corresponding, alternate interior, and same-side interior angles.

Example: Corresponding angles are equal when lines are parallel.

Triangle angles

The sum of interior angles in any triangle is 180 degrees.

Example: If two angles are 50 and 60, the third is 70.

Unit 12: Triangle side lengths

Constructing triangles

The triangle inequality says the sum of two sides must be greater than the third.

Example: Sides 2, 3, and 6 do not form a triangle.

Pythagorean theorem

In a right triangle, a^2 + b^2 = c^2.

Example: 3^2 + 4^2 = 5^2.

Distance between points

Distance formula: d = sqrt((x2 - x1)^2 + (y2 - y1)^2).

Example: Distance between (0,0) and (3,4) is 5.

Unit 13: Geometric transformations

Intro to transformations

Transformations are operations that move or resize geometric figures. They preserve certain properties and are fundamental to understanding congruence and similarity. The four main types are:

  • Translations (shifts): Move every point the same distance in the same direction
  • Rotations (turns): Turn a figure around a fixed center point by a specified angle
  • Reflections (flips): Mirror a figure across a line of reflection
  • Dilations (resizing): Enlarge or reduce a figure from a center point by a scale factor

The first three types (translations, rotations, reflections) are rigid transformations because they preserve distance and angle measures.

Example: Sliding a triangle 5 units to the right and 3 units up is a translation that preserves all side lengths and angles.

Translations

A translation (or shift) moves every point of a figure the same distance in the same direction. This preserves the shape, size, and orientation of the original figure. Translations can be described using vectors or by specifying how far to move right/left and up/down.

Important: Translations are rigid transformations — all distances and angles remain unchanged.

Example: If vertex A is at (1, 2) and we translate 3 units right and 2 units down, the new position is (1+3, 2-2) = (4, 0). If point B is at (3, 5), it moves to (6, 3).

Rotations

A rotation turns a figure around a fixed point (called the center of rotation) by a specified angle. The direction of rotation is usually measured in degrees, rotating counterclockwise by convention (negative angles rotate clockwise). Every point moves the same angular distance from the center, and the distance from each point to the center remains unchanged.

Special rotations:

  • 90° rotation: Quarter turn
  • 180° rotation: Half turn
  • 270° rotation: Three-quarter turn
  • 360° rotation: Full turn (returns to original)

Important: Rotations preserve all distances and angles, making them rigid transformations.

Example: A 90° counterclockwise rotation of point (3, 0) about the origin moves it to (0, 3). A 180° rotation of (2, 1) about the origin moves it to (-2, -1).

Reflections

A reflection flips a figure across a line called the line of reflection. Points on the line of reflection stay fixed, while all other points move to the opposite side of the line, equidistant from it. The result is a mirror image of the original figure.

Common lines of reflection:

  • Reflection across the x-axis: (x, y) → (x, -y)
  • Reflection across the y-axis: (x, y) → (-x, y)
  • Reflection across y = x: (x, y) → (y, x)
  • Reflection across any vertical or horizontal line: Mirror across that line

Important: Reflections are rigid transformations that preserve distances and angles but reverse orientation (left becomes right, clockwise becomes counterclockwise).

Example: Reflecting triangle with vertices (0,0), (2,0), (2,1) across the y-axis gives (0,0), (-2,0), (-2,1).

Properties and definitions of transformations

Rigid transformations preserve all distances and angle measures, meaning the image is congruent to the original. Non-rigid transformations may change distances or angles.

Types of transformations:

  • Rigid (isometric): Preserve all distances and angles. Translations, rotations, and reflections are rigid. Any composition of these is also rigid.
  • Non-rigid: Change distances or angles. Dilations are non-rigid because they change size.

Key invariants under rigid transformations:

  • Side lengths remain the same
  • Angle measures remain the same
  • Straight lines remain straight
  • Parallel lines remain parallel
Example: A triangle with sides 3-4-5 translated, rotated, or reflected will still have sides 3-4-5. A dilation with scale factor 2 would have sides 6-8-10.

Dilations

A dilation resizes a figure by a scale factor k, measured from a fixed center of dilation. If k > 1, the figure enlarges; if 0 < k < 1, the figure shrinks; if k = 1, there is no change. Each point moves away from (or toward) the center such that its new distance equals k times its original distance.

Important properties:

  • Angle measures are preserved
  • Side lengths are multiplied by the scale factor
  • Area is multiplied by k²
  • Dilation is a non-rigid transformation because it changes size

Scale factor effects:

  • k > 1: Enlargement
  • 0 < k < 1: Reduction
  • k < 0: Dilation combined with 180° rotation
Example: Dilate a triangle with sides 2-3-4 by scale factor 3 from any center. The result has sides 6-9-12. If the original area is 3 square units, the new area is 3 × 3² = 27 square units.

Congruence and similarity

Congruent figures have the same size and shape. Similar figures have the same shape but different size. Understanding the distinction is crucial for solving geometry problems and proving relationships.

Congruence: Two figures are congruent (denoted ≅) if one can be transformed into the other using rigid transformations (translations, rotations, reflections, or combinations). Congruent figures have:

  • Matching side lengths
  • Matching angle measures
  • Same perimeter
  • Same area

Similarity: Two figures are similar (denoted ~) if their corresponding angles are equal and their corresponding sides are proportional. Similar figures have:

  • Matching angle measures
  • Proportional side lengths (with constant ratio k)
  • Ratio of perimeters equals k
  • Ratio of areas equals k²

Relationship: Congruence is a special case of similarity where k = 1.

Example: A 3-4-5 triangle and a 6-8-10 triangle are similar with scale factor 2 (ratio of corresponding sides). They are NOT congruent because sizes differ. A 3-4-5 triangle and another 3-4-5 triangle are congruent.
